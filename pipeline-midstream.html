The bottleneck

Pipelines don't respect county lines. Neither should your research.

01 — One pipeline, four county clerks

Four filing systems, four indexing conventions, one deadline.

Linear projects cross jurisdictions by definition. Your team is pulling documents from four courthouses for one route, each with different indexing, different online portals, and different clerks who take personal days on different Fridays.

02 — Existing easement spaghetti

Old oil and gas lines, prior pipelines, and a solar option too.

Landowners have decades of prior instruments on their parcel: oil and gas leases, existing ROW grants, telecom easements, utility assignments. Your agent needs to know every one of them before they knock on the door.

03 — Compensation history matters

What did the neighbor get paid on the last pipeline?

Prior easement rates anchor the negotiation. Walking in blind means a weaker opening offer and a landowner who's already talked to three neighbors about what they got paid. Comparable history is leverage.

04 — Route changes on short notice

Engineering moves the centerline 400 feet. Now what?

A reroute triggered by wetlands, archaeology, or a landowner refusal rolls a new set of parcels into scope with the original deadline still intact. You can't re-staff in two days. Automated re-pull can.

05 — Surface use, subsurface conflict

The oil and gas operator isn't going to like this easement.

Producing leases, gathering lines, and mineral lessee consents overlap with your proposed ROW. Identifying every impacted third party up front is how you avoid construction-halt surprises six months in.

06 — Condemnation documentation

If negotiation fails, your file has to be bulletproof.

Eminent domain proceedings demand a complete title trail, prior offers on the record, and evidence of good-faith negotiation. Incomplete files lose condemnation hearings. Ours are built for the courthouse from day one.
